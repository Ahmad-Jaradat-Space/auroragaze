# date=2022-02-03 kp_peak=5 bz_min=-15 dst_min=-66 source=https://www.swpc.noaa.gov/news/swpc-statement-loss-spacex-starlink-satellites event_type=storm persona=satellite

February 2022 Starlink loss. SpaceX launched a batch of 49 Starlink satellites
on February 3, 2022 into a low parking orbit (~210 km altitude). On February 4
a moderate (G1-G2) geomagnetic storm arrived at Earth. Kp peaked at 5. The
storm was small in magnitude but its timing was the issue: it raised the
density of the upper thermosphere by an estimated 50 percent at the satellites'
parking altitude, increasing atmospheric drag substantially.

SpaceX placed the satellites into safe-mode (edge-on flight orientation to
minimise drag) but the increased drag exceeded what safe-mode could compensate.
38 of the 49 satellites failed to raise their orbits and re-entered the
atmosphere within days.

Reference frame: this event is the canonical case for "small storm, big
operational impact." It is the anchor case for satellite operators who need
storm-time density forecasts translated into actions like deferring orbit
raises or extending parking-orbit altitudes. NOAA SWPC published a statement
on February 8, 2022 noting the operational lesson.

Operational lessons:
- A G1-G2 storm is non-trivial for very-low-orbit deployments.
- Density at deployment altitude is the operationally relevant metric, not Kp alone.
- Safe-mode flight orientation is necessary but not always sufficient.
- Storm-time briefings should include drag delta at deployment altitude.
