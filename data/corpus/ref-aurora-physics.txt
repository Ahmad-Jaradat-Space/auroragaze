Aurora physics, brief reference. The aurora forms when energetic charged
particles from the magnetosphere precipitate into the upper atmosphere
along magnetic field lines and excite atmospheric atoms and molecules,
which then emit characteristic colours: green from atomic oxygen at
~100-150 km altitude, red from atomic oxygen at higher altitudes
(~150-300 km), blue and purple from molecular nitrogen at lower altitudes.

The auroral oval is a ring of precipitation centred roughly on each
geomagnetic pole. During quiet conditions the southern oval sits near
67°S geomagnetic latitude. During geomagnetic storms the oval expands
equatorward in proportion to Kp.

Visibility from the ground requires:
- The oval (or its equatorward boundary) to be near or above your horizon.
- A dark sky (no city lights).
- Clear weather and a low moon.
- Open horizon to the south (in the southern hemisphere).

Naked-eye visibility threshold:
- Overhead aurora when you are inside the oval.
- Horizon glow when the oval's equatorward boundary is within ~5 to 10 degrees
  poleward of your latitude.
- Photographable with a long exposure even when below naked-eye threshold.

Reference frame: aurora briefings should distinguish "naked-eye visible"
from "photographable," because photographic visibility extends roughly 5
to 15 degrees further equatorward than naked-eye visibility depending on
Kp and brightness.
