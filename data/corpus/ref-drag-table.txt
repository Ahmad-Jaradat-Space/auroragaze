Atmospheric density and satellite drag during geomagnetic storms.

The thermospheric density at LEO altitudes (200 to 800 km) increases
during geomagnetic storms in proportion to the energy deposited by the
auroral electrojet and the Joule heating of the upper atmosphere. Empirical
estimates from NOAA SWPC and the JB2008 model give the following indicative
density-increase ranges versus storm strength at common LEO altitudes:

- 300 km altitude:
  - G1 (Kp 5): density +20 to +30%
  - G3 (Kp 7): +50 to +80%
  - G5 (Kp 9): +200 to +400% during peak hours
- 500 km altitude:
  - G1: +5 to +10%
  - G3: +15 to +30%
  - G5: +60 to +150%
- 800 km altitude:
  - G1: +2 to +5%
  - G3: +8 to +15%
  - G5: +25 to +60%

Drag delta-v is approximately proportional to density times v^2 times
ballistic coefficient. For a typical Starlink-class satellite at 350 km:
- G1 storm timing during deployment can increase drag delta-v over 24
  hours by enough to prevent successful orbit raising. This is the Feb
  2022 lesson.
- G3+ storms during sustained station-keeping require extra propellant
  budget on the order of 10 to 40 percent of nominal weekly burn.

Operational implication: density at the satellite altitude is the
operationally relevant quantity, not Kp alone. Two storms of equal Kp can
have very different drag impacts depending on duration, ring-current
recovery, and altitude.
