Common spacecraft anomaly modes during geomagnetic storms.

1. Single-event upset (SEU). Energetic protons or heavy ions cause logic
   bit flips in onboard computers or memory. Frequent during solar
   particle events and at high-latitude passes during G3+ storms.
   Mitigation: error-correcting codes, watchdog timers, redundant logic.

2. Surface charging. Differential charging of spacecraft surfaces during
   substorms; arc discharges damage insulation, bias sensors, and degrade
   solar arrays. GEO and MEO satellites are most affected.

3. Deep-dielectric charging. High-energy electrons (MeV) penetrate
   spacecraft skin and accumulate in dielectric materials over days;
   eventual discharge can damage onboard electronics. Multi-day storm
   exposure is the trigger.

4. Atmospheric drag enhancement. Thermospheric density rises during
   storms, increasing drag at LEO altitudes. Operationally relevant
   from below ~600 km and severe below 400 km. The Feb 2022 Starlink
   loss is the canonical case.

5. Attitude-control degradation. Star-tracker dazzling by solar
   particle events, magnetometer-based attitude error during disturbed
   geomagnetic field, sun-sensor flares.

6. Solar array degradation. Particle-induced damage to solar cells; over
   the course of a single severe storm, end-of-life loss of array output
   can be accelerated by a measurable amount.

7. Communication outages. Polar-cap absorption during solar particle
   events disrupts HF and VHF; ionospheric scintillation degrades GNSS
   carriers and S-band telemetry.

Reference frame: a satellite-side briefing should map the current storm
profile to the most-likely anomaly modes for the operator's specific
mission profile, not list all of them generically.
